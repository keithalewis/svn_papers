Mortgage-Backed Securities

Mortgage-backed securities are instruments backed by pools of residential or commercial mortgages. Banks and other mortgage originators sell the loans to governmental or private entities that resell pro-rata share of all principal and interest payments on the mortgages. There are also more complicated structures such as collateralized mortgage obligations that issue tranches of the cash flows that have varying risk characteristics.

The primary risk for MBS investors comes from prepayments. When interest rates fall, borrowers tend to pay off their mortgage and the principal is returned in a less attractive reinvestment environment. For unsecured mortgages there is also a significant credit risk due to default.

Terminology

securitization
Pooling assets used for producing cash flows.
prepayment
Option held by mortgage owner to pay off outstanding principal.
prime
Fully vetted borrowers.
subprime
Borrowers with weak credit ratings.
alt-A
Non-conforming but generally good credit borrowers.
jumbo
Loan exceeds assets.
ARM
Adjustable Rate Mortgage. Interest rate changes over the life of the mortgage, often based on 1-year constant-maturity Treasury rates, COFI, or LIBOR.
balloon
Mortgage that does not fully amortize hence has a large balloon principal payment due at the termination of the mortgage.
turnover
Prepayment due to selling a house.
curtailment
Partial prepayment of mortgage principal.
burnout
The tendency for seasoned mortgage pools to to prepay at a slower rate.
pass-though
All principal and interest payments are pro-rata distributed to MBS owners.
