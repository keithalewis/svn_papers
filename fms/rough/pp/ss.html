Sample Spaces

A sample space is the set of possible outcomes in a probability model. Elements of the sample space are called outcomes.

When modeling a coin flip, the sample space could be \(\{H, T\}\) where \(H\) represents the outcome of flipping heads and \(T\) represents the outcome of flipping tails. We could also, if somwhat perversely, let \(H\) denote tails and \(T\) denote heads. The choice of sample space is not unique.

When modeling a six sided die the sample space could be \(\{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\}\). Another possible sample space is the collection of intervals \(\{[0,1/6), [1/6, 1/3),\dots, [5/6, 1)\}\), where the outcome rolling a face with \(j\) pips corresponds to the interval \([(j-1)/6, j/6)\).

When modeling Brownian motion the customary sample space is \(C([0,\infty))\), the space of continuous real-valued functions on the non-negative real numbers. The outcome \(\omega\in C([0,\infty))\) represents the path \(t\mapsto\omega(t)\).
