Independence

Two algebras, \({\cal A}\) and \({\cal B}\), are independent under the probability measure \(P\) if \(P(A\cap B) = P(A) P(B)\) for every \(A\in{\cal A}\) and \(B\in{\cal B}\). A random variable \(X\) is independent of an algebra \({\cal A}\) if the smallest algebra making \(X\) measurable is independent of \({\cal A}\). Two random variables are independent if the smallest algebras under which they are measurable are independent.

One nice feature of independent random variables is that their marginal distributions determine their joint distribution. If \(X\) has cdf \(F\) and \(Y\) has cdf \(G\) then their joint distribution is \(H(x,y) = P(X\le x,Y\le y) = F(x)G(y)\).

Copulas

Maybe this section should be titled Dependence. Copulas contain precisely the information required to connect marginal distributions into joint distributions. With notation as above, the copula of \(X\) and \(Y\) is the joint distribution of \(F(X)\) and \(G(Y)\), \(C(u, v) = P(F(X)\le u, G(Y)\le v)\), \(0\le u,v\le 1\). Recall both of these random variables are uniformly distributed on \([0,1]\).

Exercise. With \(F\), \(G\), \(H\) and \(C\) as above, show \(H(x,y) = C(F(x),G(y))\).

Exercise. If \(X\) and \(Y\) are independent, show \(C(u, v) = uv\).

Exercise. If \(Y = \phi(X)\) where \(\phi\) is an increasing function, show \(C(u, v) = \min\{u,v\} =: M(u,v)\).

Exercise. If \(Y = \phi(X)\) where \(\phi\) is a decreasing function, show \(C(u, v) = \max\{u + v - 1, 0\} =: W(u,v)\).

Exercise. (Fréchet–Hoeffding bounds) For any copula \(C\), show \(W\le C\le M\).
