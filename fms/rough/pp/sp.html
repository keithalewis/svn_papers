Stochastic Processes

A stochastic process is a collection of random variables and algebras indexed by time, \((X_t)_{t\in T}\) and \(({\cal A}_t)_{t\in T}\), where \(T\subseteq [0,\infty)\). Just as a random variables are determined by their joint distribution, a stochastic process is completely determined by its finite joint distributions. Given \(t_0\lt\cdots\lt t_n\) in \(T\) the corresponding finite joint distribution is \(F(x_0,\dots,x_n) = P(X_{t_0}\le x_0,\dots,X_{t_n}\le x_n)\).

A random process is adapted if \(X_t\) is \({\cal A}_t\) measurable for every \(t\in T\). If we consider \({\cal A}_t\) to be the partial information available at time \(t\) then this means the process does not 'look into the future.'

Typically the algebras have the property \({\cal A}_t\subseteq{\cal A}_u\) if \(t\lt u\) to reflect the notion that information increases over time. We do not make that assumption in general to reflect that under some conditions the only information required at time \(t\) is the currently information and that the historical information is not required. This is often the case with Markov processes or binomial tree models.

Martingales

A martingale is a stochastic process with the property \(E[X_u|{\cal A}_t] = X_t\) for \(t\lt u\). Processes having independent increments are martingales. A simple way to produce martingales is to fix a random variable \(X\) and define \(X_t = E[X|{\cal A}_t]\). This is a martingale by the tower property of conditional expectation, but not all martingales can be expressed this way.

Remarks

The Fundamental Theorem of Asset Pricing states arbitrage free models of prices have a positive scalar muliplier (price deflator) making them into martingales.

Futures quotes are naturally occuring martingales because the price of a futures contract is always zero.

There is a deep connection between martingales and harmonic functions as first shown by Kakutani in 1944 for the case of 2-dimensional Brownian motion.
