Basic Facts about Futures

A futures contract is specified by an underlying index and an expiration date. Prior to the the expiration the price is whatever the market says it is.

The actual mechanics of trading a futures contract is that it costs nothing to enter into the contract, modulo setting up an account with a broker and providing an initial margin. You get paid the change in the market quoted price each day until your margin is used up. Then your broker will take you out of your position, usually at a price unfavorable to you.

The mathematical idealization of this is that the quoted price of a futures is a martingale.

Let \(F\) be the value of the futures at expiration having cumulative distribution function \(\Phi(x) = P(F\le x)\). and \(f = E F\). We assume \(F\ge0\). \(p(k) = E(k -F)^+\), \(c(k) = E(F - k)^+\), \(p(0) = 0\), \(c(0) = EF\). \(p(0) = 0\), \(c(k) - (f - k)\to 0\) as \(k\to\infty\). \(p(k) - c(k) = k - EF\), \(p'(k) - c'(k) = 1\). \(p'(k) = P(F\le k) = \Phi(k)\), \(c'(k) = -P(F\ge k) = \Phi(k) - 1\). \(p'(0) = 0\), \(c'(0) = 1\). \(p'(k) \ge 0\), \(c'(k) \le 0\), \(p''(k) = c''(k) = \Phi'(k) \ge 0\).

\(E(F - a)^2 = 2\int_0^a p(k)\,dk + 2\int_a^\infty c(k)\,dk\).
